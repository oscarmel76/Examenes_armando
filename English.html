Edison's Best Invention

Module 8 · Pages 250–262

📖 Story Summary

This text is about Thomas Edison, one of the greatest inventors in history. Edison was known for creating many useful things, but his best invention was the phonograph — a machine that could record and play back sound for the very first time.

Edison worked hard in his laboratory with his team. He believed that with enough effort, any problem could be solved. The text shows how Edison thought differently from others and never gave up, even when things were difficult.

The story teaches us that great ideas come from curiosity, hard work, and creativity.

💡 Big Idea: Edison's visionary thinking and hard work changed the world forever.

📝 Vocabulary Words

Visionary
Definition
A person who has big, creative ideas about the future.
Example
"Thomas Edison was a visionary because he imagined inventions nobody had ever seen before."
Compact
Definition
Small in size but complete and well organized.
Example
"The new music player was compact, so it fit easily in a backpack."
Singles
Definition
Individual items or songs, one at a time.
Example
"You could buy singles of your favorite songs instead of the whole album."
Memorable
Definition
Something so special that you remember it for a long time.
Example
"Hearing recorded music for the first time was a memorable moment in history."
System
Definition
A group of parts that work together to do something.
Example
"Edison created a system that could capture sound and play it back."
Innovative
Definition
New, creative, and different from what existed before.
Example
"The phonograph was innovative because no one had ever built anything like it."

How Did That Get Into My Lunchbag?

Module 9 · Pages 276–292

📖 Story Summary

This text explains where our food comes from before it arrives in our lunchbox. Many kids don't think about how food is made — they just eat it!

The story traces different foods back to their origins on farms, in fields, and in factories. For example, cheese comes from milk, milk comes from cows, and cows eat grass on a farm.

The text teaches us that food goes through a long journey — from nature, to workers, to trucks, to stores, and finally to our lunch bags.

🌿 Big Idea: Every food in your lunchbag has a long and amazing journey before reaching you.

📝 Vocabulary Words

Stalk
Definition
The tall, thick stem of a plant.
Example
"The corn grew on a long green stalk in the field."
Dairy
Definition
Products made from milk, like cheese, butter, and yogurt.
Example
"Milk and cheese are dairy products that come from cows."
Curds
Definition
The thick, solid parts that form when milk is being made into cheese.
Example
"The farmer separated the curds from the liquid to make fresh cheese."
Tingly
Definition
A feeling of slight excitement or a light stinging sensation.
Example
"The sour candy made her tongue feel tingly."
Scarlet
Definition
A bright, deep shade of red.
Example
"The tomatoes were scarlet red and ready to be picked."
Grove
Definition
A small group of trees growing together.
Example
"The orange grove was full of ripe fruit ready for harvest."

It's Our Garden

Module 9 · Pages 314–332 · From Seeds to Harvest

📖 Story Summary

This text is about a school garden project where students learn to grow their own food from start to finish. The students work together to plan, plant, care for, and harvest vegetables and plants.

The story shows each step — from choosing where to plant, to preparing the soil, to watching seeds grow into full plants. The students learn about teamwork, responsibility, and patience.

The text teaches us that growing food takes planning and care, and that working together makes it more fun and successful.

🌻 Big Idea: A school garden teaches students to work together and care for living things.

📝 Vocabulary Words

Layout
Definition
The way things are arranged or organized in a space.
Example
"The students planned the layout of the garden so each plant had enough room."
Arbor
Definition
A shaded area in a garden formed by plants or wood overhead.
Example
"The students sat under the arbor to eat lunch in the shade."
Transplanted
Definition
Moved a plant from one place and replanted it in another.
Example
"They transplanted the small tomato plants from pots into the garden soil."
Mulch
Definition
A layer of leaves or wood chips placed on soil to protect plants.
Example
"The students spread mulch around the plants to keep the soil from drying out."
Blooming
Definition
When flowers or plants are opening and growing beautifully.
Example
"By spring, the garden was blooming with colorful flowers and green vegetables."
Kernels
Definition
The small, individual seeds or grains of a plant, like corn.
Example
"Each kernel of corn on the cob could grow into a new corn plant."

The Twenty-One Balloons

Chapters 1–5 · William Pène du Bois

👤 Main Characters

Prof. William W. Sherman
The main character. A retired math teacher who loves peace and quiet. He travels alone in a hot air balloon.
The Krakatoan Families
A secret community of 20 families living on Krakatoa. They are rich, creative, and well organized.
Mr. F (Mr. Felix)
One of the leaders of the community. He is the first to welcome Professor Sherman to the island.

🗺️ Important Settings

🌉 San Francisco
Where Professor Sherman begins his balloon journey across the Pacific Ocean.
🌊 The Pacific Ocean
Where the Professor travels and has his accident with the seagull.
🌋 Krakatoa Island
The secret island where he lands and discovers an amazing hidden civilization full of diamonds.

📖 Chapter Summaries

Chapter 1 — The Professor's Plan
Professor Sherman is tired of teaching and wants adventure and solitude. He buys a large hot air balloon and plans to float across the Pacific Ocean for one year. He says goodbye to San Francisco and takes off alone.
💡 Key Idea: The Professor wants peace, freedom, and adventure.
Chapter 2 — Trouble Over the Ocean
While flying over the Pacific Ocean, a seagull crashes into the balloon and damages it. Professor Sherman is forced to make an emergency landing on an island — which turns out to be Krakatoa.
💡 Key Idea: An accident completely changes the Professor's plans.
Chapter 3 — The Secret Island
Professor Sherman discovers that Krakatoa is not empty. A secret community of 20 families lives there, hidden from the world. The island is beautiful and advanced. The families are very wealthy because Krakatoa is full of diamonds.
💡 Key Idea: Krakatoa hides an extraordinary secret civilization.
Chapter 4 — The Rules of Krakatoa
The Professor learns the rules of the island. Each family runs a restaurant with food from a different country, and residents eat at a different one each day. The community is creative and happy. The Professor is welcomed but told he cannot leave easily.
💡 Key Idea: The island has a unique and creative way of living together.
Chapter 5 — Life on the Island
Professor Sherman experiences daily life on Krakatoa. He enjoys the food, culture, and people. He is amazed by how the families built such an organized life in secret. However, he starts to feel tension — the volcano beneath the island is active and dangerous.
💡 Key Idea: Life on the island is wonderful, but a dangerous secret hides underneath.
